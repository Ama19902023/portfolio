## The deadline most actuaries are ignoring

On 2 December 2027, the EU AI Act fully applies to autonomous high-risk AI systems (Annex III). On 2 August 2028, it extends to AI embedded in regulated products (Annex I).

For the insurance industry, this is not a distant regulatory concern. It is a concrete operational deadline that affects pricing models, reserving algorithms, underwriting systems and — potentially — any actuarial model that makes or influences decisions about individuals.

Most actuaries I talk to are not aware of this. Most IT departments know the AI Act exists but do not understand which models are affected. The gap between regulatory text and operational reality is exactly where practitioners like me work.

## What is the AI Act?

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation 2024/1689) is the first comprehensive legal framework for AI systems in the world. It uses a risk-based approach:

- **Unacceptable risk**: Banned outright (social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance)
- **High risk**: Allowed but with strict requirements (documentation, human oversight, accuracy, robustness)
- **Limited risk**: Transparency obligations only
- **Minimal risk**: No specific obligations

The critical question for actuaries: **are our models high-risk?**

## Which actuarial models are high-risk?

Annex III of the AI Act lists categories of high-risk AI systems. The most relevant for insurance:

**Article 6(2) — AI systems used in:**

- Access to private services and public benefits
- Evaluation of creditworthiness
- Risk assessment and pricing in life and health insurance

This last point is explicit: **AI systems used for risk assessment and pricing in life and health insurance are high-risk under the AI Act.**

This covers:
- ML pricing models (GLM, LightGBM, XGBoost) for health, life, protection
- Scoring models for underwriting acceptance/rejection
- Automated claims assessment systems
- Any model that influences individual insurance decisions

**What about reserving and ALM models?**

The situation is less clear. IBNR models, BEL calculations and SCR models do not directly make decisions about individuals — they aggregate portfolio-level estimates. The regulatory interpretation is still evolving.

However, if a reserving model feeds into a decision that affects individual policyholders (claims settlement, contract renewal), it may be in scope.

My reading: be conservative. If your model touches individual decisions, assume it is high-risk until EIOPA publishes clearer guidance.

## What high-risk AI systems must do

High-risk AI systems under the AI Act must comply with requirements in Articles 8-15:

**1. Risk management system (Article 9)**
A documented process to identify, analyse and mitigate risks throughout the model lifecycle. This is similar to model risk management frameworks already required by some prudential supervisors — but now it is law.

**2. Data governance (Article 10)**
Training, validation and testing data must be documented. Relevant for ML pricing models: you need to show your training data is representative, free of discriminatory bias, and appropriate for the intended use.

**3. Technical documentation (Article 11)**
Before deployment, a technical file must be prepared covering model architecture, training data, performance metrics, known limitations. Think of it as a mandatory model card.

**4. Transparency and logging (Article 12)**
Automatic logging of operations. For pricing models: logs of which version of the model produced which output, when, and for whom.

**5. Human oversight (Article 14)**
High-risk AI systems must be designed to allow human oversight. Fully automated decisions without human review are problematic. For actuarial models: underwriters must be able to override model outputs.

**6. Accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity (Article 15)**
Models must be tested for accuracy across different population segments. Performance degradation over time must be monitored.

## The convergence with Solvency II and IFRS 17

Here is where it gets interesting for practitioners.

**Solvency II 2027 revision** requires enhanced model documentation and governance for internal models. 

**IFRS 17** requires explicit documentation of actuarial assumptions and methodology.

**AI Act** requires technical documentation, data governance and human oversight for high-risk AI.

These three frameworks are converging toward the same objective: **traceable, auditable, explainable models with human oversight**. The good news is that a well-designed model governance framework can satisfy all three simultaneously.

This is the insight most organisations are missing: they are building three separate compliance processes when one unified framework would do.

## What you need to do before December 2027

**Step 1 — Inventory your AI systems**
List all models that make or influence decisions about individuals. For each: model type, training data, decision scope, current documentation level.

**Step 2 — Risk classification**
Apply the Annex III criteria. Flag models likely to be high-risk. When in doubt, consult legal counsel familiar with the AI Act.

**Step 3 — Gap analysis**
For each high-risk model, assess compliance against Articles 8-15. Where are the gaps? Missing documentation? No logging? No human override mechanism?

**Step 4 — Remediation roadmap**
Build a project plan to close the gaps before December 2027. This is a significant undertaking — start now.

**Step 5 — Governance framework**
Design or adapt your model risk management framework to cover AI Act requirements. Integrate with existing Solvency II and IFRS 17 governance where possible.
