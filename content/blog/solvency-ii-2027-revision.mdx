## Why 2027 is different from 2016

Solvency II entered into force in January 2016. For most European actuaries, that implementation project is now ancient history — the framework is embedded, the processes are running, the ratios are reported quarterly.

So when regulators announced a revision, many teams assumed it would be cosmetic adjustments at the margins.

It is not.

The revised Solvency II Directive (transposition deadline: 30 January 2027) introduces significant changes in four areas: proportionality, long-term guarantee measures, reporting, and sustainability. For practitioners who have been running SII processes for 10 years, several of these changes require genuine rework.

Here is what actually matters.

## Change 1 — Proportionality: smaller insurers get relief, larger ones get more scrutiny

The revision substantially expands the proportionality regime — the set of simplifications available to smaller, less complex insurers.

**What changes:**
- Expanded definition of "small and non-complex" undertakings
- Simplified SCR calculation options for eligible firms
- Reduced reporting requirements (fewer QRTs)
- Lighter ORSA requirements

**What this means in practice:**
For large insurers like BNP Paribas Cardif, proportionality relief is irrelevant — you are not eligible. But the flip side is that regulators are using the proportionality expansion to justify tighter requirements on large, complex firms that benefit from internal models.

Expect more scrutiny on internal model validation, more detailed documentation requirements, and higher expectations on model governance — which aligns directly with AI Act requirements.

## Change 2 — Long-term guarantee measures: recalibration

The Volatility Adjustment (VA) and Matching Adjustment (MA) are recalibrated.

**Volatility Adjustment changes:**
- The VA calculation methodology is revised to better reflect insurer-specific asset allocations
- A new "macro VA" component is introduced for systemic market stress periods
- Country-specific VAs are adjusted

**What this means in practice:**
For life insurers with significant EUR sovereign bond portfolios, the VA change can meaningfully affect BEL — and therefore SCR ratio. This requires actuarial teams to recompute BEL sensitivities to VA under the new methodology.

The technical implementation depends on EIOPA's revised delegated acts, expected in late 2026. Teams should be preparing the methodology now, not waiting for the final text.

**Matching Adjustment changes:**
- Eligibility criteria are slightly relaxed
- New asset classes may become eligible (infrastructure debt, green bonds)
- Reporting on MA portfolios is enhanced

For insurers currently using MA, this is an opportunity — more eligible assets means potentially higher MA, lower BEL, better ratio. But the enhanced reporting is a burden.

## Change 3 — Reporting: more granularity, new QRTs

The QRT (Quantitative Reporting Template) framework is revised. Key changes:

**New or revised QRTs:**
- Enhanced climate risk reporting (physical and transition risk)
- Revised own funds templates with more granularity on tier classification
- New templates for proportionality regime eligibility assessment
- Enhanced SCR templates for market risk sub-modules

**XBRL mandate:**
The revision accelerates the move to XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language) for all QRT submissions. If your reporting pipeline still produces Excel-to-QRT conversions, this is a significant IT project.

**What this means in practice:**
Reporting teams need to audit their current QRT production process against the new templates — expected from EIOPA in H1 2026. The XBRL transition alone is a 6-12 month IT project for most large insurers.

## Change 4 — Sustainability: climate risk becomes quantitative

This is the most structurally new element. The revision formally integrates climate risk into the SII framework:

**Scenario analysis:**
Insurers above a size threshold must perform quantitative climate scenario analysis — physical risk (floods, droughts, heatwaves) and transition risk (carbon price shock, stranded assets).

**SCR implications:**
Climate risk does not yet have a dedicated SCR sub-module in the standard formula — but EIOPA is developing one, expected post-2027. Internal model users are expected to integrate climate risk into their capital models.

**ORSA implications:**
The ORSA must explicitly cover climate risk — with quantitative scenario analysis, not just qualitative discussion.

**What this means in practice:**
This is new territory for most actuarial teams. Climate scenario analysis requires:
- Access to climate data (physical hazard maps, transition scenarios)
- Economic models linking climate outcomes to insurance losses
- New actuarial methodologies for long-horizon projection

The intersection of climate risk and actuarial modelling is an emerging specialty. Teams that develop this capability early will have a significant competitive advantage.

## The convergence with IFRS 17

A deliberate objective of the SII revision is greater convergence with IFRS 17 — particularly on discount rates and risk adjustment.

**Discount rates:**
The SII risk-free rate curve and the IFRS 17 discount curve are methodologically similar but not identical. The revision moves them closer — reducing the operational burden of maintaining two separate curve construction processes.

**Risk margin vs Risk Adjustment:**
The SII Risk Margin (RM) and the IFRS 17 Risk Adjustment (RA) serve similar conceptual purposes but are calculated differently. The revision updates the RM methodology (cost-of-capital rate from 6% to 4.75%) — making the gap with IFRS 17 RA slightly smaller.

For actuarial teams maintaining both SII and IFRS 17 calculations, this convergence is welcome — but it does not eliminate the need for two separate calculations.

## What Swiss practitioners need to know

For actuaries targeting Switzerland, the SII revision is relevant in two ways:

**1. Swiss subsidiaries of EU groups**
Many Swiss insurers have EU subsidiaries subject to SII. The revision affects these entities directly.

**2. SST convergence**
FINMA is watching the SII revision carefully. The Swiss Solvency Test (SST) and SII have been converging for years — the 2027 revision continues this trend. Expect FINMA to publish updated SST guidance that reflects similar themes: climate risk, proportionality, enhanced governance.

For a practitioner with both SII and SST knowledge, the 2027 revision reinforces the value of cross-framework expertise.
